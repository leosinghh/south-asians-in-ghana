Historical timeline

This page focuses on the chronology most strongly supported by Ghana-focused scholarship: late colonial arrival, interwar merchant settlement, post-Partition family migration, post-independence commercial growth, and later policy shocks.

1890

Community histories and later scholarship identify Bhai Bhoolchand as a landmark early Sindhi arrival in the Gold Coast. This date is important because it anchors the local memory of the community’s beginnings.

1931

Ioné Acquah’s Accra social survey recorded 21 “Indians,” described as male merchants. This confirms a small but visible pre-independence South Asian presence in urban commercial life.

1939

Later community narratives describe family migration becoming more established by this period, including the better-remembered early family histories connected to firms such as the Mahtanis.

1947–1948

The Partition of British India reshaped the Sindhi diaspora globally. In Ghana’s case, scholarship shows that the late 1940s marked a significant phase of family-based settlement rather than only male trading migration.

1950s–1960s

Some Sindhi firms expanded beyond retail and wholesale into manufacturing, especially garments and consumer goods. This period is key for understanding how commercial communities became tied to postcolonial development.

1969

The Aliens Compliance Order became one of the major turning points in Ghana’s migration history, tightening enforcement around residence status and making foreign communities more vulnerable.

1970

The Ghanaian Business (Promotion) Act, 1970, reserved parts of commerce, especially retail trade, for Ghanaians. It reshaped who could legally operate in which spaces of the economy.

1975

The Hindu Monastery of Africa was founded in Accra. Its significance goes beyond diaspora religion because it reflects a Ghanaian-led Hindu institutional history that also interacted with Indian migrants and gurus.

1979

Scholarship on race and citizenship in Ghana shows renewed registration drives and deportation pressures affecting “non-African” populations, including Indians, during the AFRC period.

2021

Ghana’s census reported 171,490 non-Ghanaians, including 11,313 from Asia. The number is useful as an official baseline, but it does not map neatly onto “South Asians in Ghana” because the category is broader than the community itself.

Key takeaway

The Ghana case is not one of a massive single migration wave. It is a layered history of small beginnings, family-based consolidation, commercial expansion, and political vulnerability.

Why this matters historically

It helps explain how minority commercial groups can become deeply rooted in a nation’s history while still being treated, at moments of crisis, as outsiders.
