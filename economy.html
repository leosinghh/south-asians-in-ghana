Trade, enterprise, and state power

Much of the best Ghana-specific literature shows South Asians in Ghana first through commerce: import-export trade, retail, wholesale, and later manufacturing. But this same visibility also made them legible targets in moments of economic nationalism.

Commerce first

Early South Asian commercial activity in the Gold Coast is most strongly associated with shopkeeping, wholesale, textiles, and imported consumer goods in urban trading centres.

Manufacturing later

By the 1950s and 1960s, some firms moved into industrial production. This matters because it complicates the stereotype of the minority trader as only a “middleman.”

Embeddedness

These businesses were not outside Ghanaian life. They employed workers, used local urban space, paid taxes and licenses, and shaped consumer geography in cities such as Accra.

Examples of firms named in community and scholarly histories

Metharam Brothers

Frequently cited among early trading firms in Gold Coast / Ghana community histories.

Bombay Bazaar

Often referenced as one of the early Indian commercial establishments in the Gold Coast.

Mahtani Brothers

Linked to family and commercial histories that illustrate the move from trade to rooted community life.

Policy shocks

1969 Aliens Compliance Order

This is one of the central legal-political turning points for understanding foreign communities in Ghana. Historians describe it as a moment of intensified enforcement that framed migrants through unemployment, security, and national control.

1970 Ghanaian Business (Promotion) Act

Act 334 restricted ownership of certain kinds of businesses, especially retail trade, to Ghanaians. This matters because it targeted economic space, not just movement across borders.

Key historical tension: the same commercial success that helped South Asian communities build durable lives in Ghana also made them visible in periods of nationalist backlash.
